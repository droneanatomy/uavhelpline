For years the counter-UAV conversation centred on individual products: a jammer here, a detection radar there. That framing is fading. The harder, more useful question operators now ask is how detection, identification, and mitigation layers fit together into a single coherent picture.

## Technical Breakdown

A layered architecture typically combines several sensing modalities so that the weaknesses of one are covered by another.

- **Radar** provides range and track but can struggle with very small, slow targets against cluttered backgrounds.
- **RF detection** identifies control and video links and can flag a drone before it is visually acquired, but is blind to fully autonomous platforms that emit nothing.
- **Electro-optical and infrared** sensors give visual confirmation and classification, but have shorter effective range and depend on line of sight and weather.

The integration layer — the software that fuses these feeds into one track and one decision — is increasingly where the real engineering value sits. Mitigation specifics are deliberately kept high level here. **[Redacted for safety]**

## Industry Impact

For **manufacturers**, the moat is shifting from individual sensors toward the fusion software and open integration interfaces that let a customer combine best-of-breed components. For **operators**, procurement is becoming a systems-design exercise rather than a shopping list. For **regulators**, layered detection raises fresh questions about spectrum use and authorisation of active measures. For **integrators**, the opportunity is clear: the buyer increasingly wants one pane of glass, not five consoles.

The takeaway is that counter-UAV maturity now looks less like a single breakthrough device and more like a well-orchestrated system.
